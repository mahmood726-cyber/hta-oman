🇴🇲 Oman Just Activated the Engine of Smarter Health Spending. HTA Guidelines Are Here — And They’re About to Reshape Everything. At OIPCX 2025, the [Ministry of Health Oman](https://om.linkedin.com/company/ministry-of-health-oman?trk=public_post-text) launched its first official Methodological Guidelines for Health Technology Assessment (HTA) — a powerful move that marks the start of evidence-based healthcare policymaking in the country. 🔍 What was implemented? ✅ A national HTA framework tailored to Oman ✅ Based on global gold standards from [ISPOR—The Professional Society for Health Economics and Outcomes Research](https://www.linkedin.com/company/ispororg?trk=public_post-text)&[EUnetHTA - European Network for Health Technology Assessment](https://nl.linkedin.com/in/eunethta?trk=public_post-text) ✅ Localized through stakeholder collaboration ✅ Built to guide how Oman chooses, purchases, and uses health technologies 💡 Why does it matter? Because until now, decisions on new drugs, diagnostics, or medical devices were often made with limited data. With HTA, Oman is now able to: ✔️ Evaluate technologies based on cost, clinical benefit, and outcomes ✔️ Prioritize what actually works — and what delivers real value ✔️ Spend smarter, not just more ✔️ Align health investments with national goals under Vision 2040 🏥 How will it affect Oman’s healthcare system? • Patients will benefit from access to proven, high-impact technologies • Hospitals and providers will gain clarity on what to adopt • The government will be empowered to allocate resources efficiently • Private sector innovators will have a roadmap for market access This isn’t a policy change. This is healthcare transformation. 👋 I’m Pinal Patel, CEO of Adhrit Consultancy. We work at the intersection of healthcare, technology, and policy — helping countries and companies navigate HTA, value-based care, and strategic growth. (2025) in Frontiers in Health Services. This pioneering framework outlines a four-phase roadmap for institutionalizing Health Technology Assessment (HTA) within Emirates Health Services (EHS) — from early strategy alignment and budget impact modeling to full-scale integration of cost-effectiveness and patient-centered metrics. 💡 Key Highlights: Establishes a structured, transparent approach to HTA across the UAE. Promotes value-based healthcare, innovation access, and financial sustainability. Strengthens stakeholder collaboration and data-driven decision-making. By embedding HTA into national health governance, the UAE continues to align with global best practices — ensuring that every healthcare innovation delivers measurable value for patients and the system alike. Canada’s healthcare sector is entering a transformational growth era. Why Healthcare Organizations in the GCC Are Transforming — and What Lies Ahead The GCC healthcare sector is transforming faster than ever — driven by competition, regulation, and the need for sustainable growth. In my latest post, I explore why transformation is no longer optional— and how consolidation, digitalization, and evolving regulations are shaping the next decade. [#HealthcareStrategy](https://www.linkedin.com/signup/cold-join?session_redirect=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.linkedin.com%2Ffeed%2Fhashtag%2Fhealthcarestrategy&trk=public_post-text)[#GCC](https://www.linkedin.com/signup/cold-join?session_redirect=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.linkedin.com%2Ffeed%2Fhashtag%2Fgcc&trk=public_post-text)[#Transformation](https://www.linkedin.com/signup/cold-join?session_redirect=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.linkedin.com%2Ffeed%2Fhashtag%2Ftransformation&trk=public_post-text)[#Leadership](https://www.linkedin.com/signup/cold-join?session_redirect=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.linkedin.com%2Ffeed%2Fhashtag%2Fleadership&trk=public_post-text) The GCC healthcare sector is at a pivotal moment. What began as a race to build world-class facilities has now evolved into a quest for sustainability, efficiency, and differentiation. 1) Rapid Vertical Growth: The region has witnessed a remarkable surge in healthcare infrastructure — from advanced tertiary hospitals to specialized centers and digital health platforms. This expansion, while impressive, has led to market saturation in several sub-segments, urging organizations to rethink their value propositions. 2) Rising Competition: Multiple hospitals, polyclinics, and niche specialty providers are now competing for the same patient base. In this environment,experience, trust, and outcomes have become the new differentiators — not just reputation or location. 3) Downward Pressure on Prices: With increasing capacity and insurance-driven negotiations, pricing power is eroding. Institutions are being forced to innovate operationally, optimize costs, and redefine service models to protect margins. 4) Post-COVID Acceleration: The pandemic acted as an inflection point — pushing digital transformation, patient empowerment, and remote care to the forefront. Healthcare delivery is now expected to be hybrid, personalized, and data-driven. 5) Evolving Regulations & Forthcoming Consolidation: As regulators across GCC countries strengthen quality frameworks, data governance, and licensing standards, a new wave of consolidation is likely to follow — where scale, integration, and efficiency will determine survival. The transformation we’re witnessing is not merely structural — it’s philosophical. The next decade will belong to healthcare organizations that treat transformation not as a response, but as a core discipline of leadership.

I attended [Dr. Elisabeth Brock](https://ch.linkedin.com/in/dr-elisabeth-brock-639b596?trk=public_post-text)'s webinar last week on the what makes Switzerland a compelling first launch market compared to other European countries such France, Germany or UK. It was thoroughly interesting and informative to hear facts about the Swiss Health system. For example, the health system ensures universal health coverage through “private not for profit insurers“ in contrast to France and Germany with 95% patient satisfaction. Although there are high premiums, there is rapid access and high level of service. Some of the main takeaways for me regarding its launch potential were: -Pricing and reimbursement processes can run in parallel to the newly launched early access pathway allowing efficiency and early engagement. -The HTA Assessment is based on the WZW criteria: Wirksamkeit (Efficacy/Safety) Zweckmässigkeit (Usefulness/Appropriateness) Wirtschaftlichkeit (Economic Efficiency) – supported by a budget Impact Model with a full cost-effectiveness analysis is not mandatory. -Dossiers must be concise (≤30 pages), submitted in German, French, or Italian, with appendices permitted in English- It cannot be a copy paste from French/ German dossiers -Local expert input is critical to align with Swiss clinical and payer expectations. -Cultural and linguistic differences across regions should inform engagement strategies- Patient care is based on a combination of French, German and Italian practices with US guidelines being referred to commonly -Swiss KOLs are highly influential at global level and contribute significantly to international evidence generation and clinical practice standards: Engagement is key to -Important to keep an eye out on the Swiss landscape as it is constantly evolving.
